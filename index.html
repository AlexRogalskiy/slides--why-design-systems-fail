Investment

-- image here of working out --

To have a successful design system, you need to make a continuous effort to invest resources into it.

- **It's like working out**. - Small incremental investments over time lead to big gains overall. - If you invest once (say, 3 months of overhauling your design system), but neglect to keep it up, you'll find immediate gratification but won't see much improvement after that. - You're probably going to lose some of the gains you made as your codebase becomes messier and starts to stray from your original design system, and then you'll find yourself in for another round of large investment, but dreading going through the process because its fallen out of shape. - Because of how they scale, design systems can really make a large impact across the platform and thus its important to really invest in things like accessibility from the start

Friction

"If its harder for people to use than their current system, people won't use it" â€”Jason Garber
You have to make your design system the path of least resistance, lowering cognitive overhead of development, not adding to it. This is vital. A design system is intended to make development much more efficient, enforce a consistent style across sites, and allow for the developer to not worry as much about small decisions like naming and HTML semantics. These are already sorted out for them, meaning they can focus on building product. But if your design system is complicated and over-engineered, they may find it frustrating to use and go back to what they know, even if what they know is tacky. If you're a Sass expert, and based your system on complex hooks and functions, you better hope your user (the developer) is also a Sass expert, or wants to learn. This is often not the case. You need to talk to your audience. This is a mistake I've personally learned from. While the system was beautiful and performant, it was over engineered to an audience that just wanted class names and structure. I can't emphasize enough how important it is for you to talk to your audience.
So with BUI v2, we provide options -- there is option 1, where users can implement it using Sass, select just the components they want to include, and extend the system using hooks. That's the most performant and extensible output. But then there is also the user who just wants to throw a link onto their page and make it look pretty. A great usecase here is a hackathon.

Buy-in

No engineer likes being told what to do in a presecriptive manner. A design systems team needs to keep this in mind. The product will be much more effective if you bring in stakeholders early on (and by stakeholders I mean the engineers that will be using the system). Ask for their input and really listen. This is a big lesson I learned lately. All the work you think is right and good means nothing if the people you are "selling" it to aren't sold. And if they have a say early on, then they become a part of your team. They want it to succeed. So never underestimate the power of buy-in.

Enforcement

But I do need to talk about enforcement of this as well. Someone has to be a decision maker here and say "okay everyone now use this design system to build". Ideally, with the engineers on your side, they will *want* to. They'll be excited to. But there are also a few tools you can put in place to help with cleanliness of code as they are implementing

Semantic Versioning

Major.Minor.Patch

- Something that really helps with implementation of design systems are keeping clean versions. - Semantic versioning is great for design systems - SemVer is where you denote any updates to the system via a system of Major.Minor.Patch - On all of the design systems I've worked on, we've implemented this - Updated a component's color? That's a minor release since it won't cause breaking changes that may affect layout or other components - Changing layout objects or markup was a Major release since this would be a breaking change, and to update to the next version, effort would need to be put into doing some visual regression testing to that things don't break
-- image of github releases --
- With github, we can link to specific version releases - For our built CDN assets, we run a deploy script that automatically created folders for each realease, as well as a `latest` folder if someone wanted the always-up-to-date version of the design system

Ownership

Ultimately, someone needs to own this. Our design system was initially under a front-end infrastructure team, but its since moved to the design org for a tighter collaboration, and we're looking into how to pull it out into its own product vertical. With this structure, we can open PRs on the system itself, but more importantly, we can also open PRs on where we see inconsistencies throughout the UI and we can allow for other people to file bugs and contribute

Contribution

I want to wrap this up by talking about a contribution cycle. As your engineers are implementing the design system, there needs to be a way for them to continue to provide feedback.
